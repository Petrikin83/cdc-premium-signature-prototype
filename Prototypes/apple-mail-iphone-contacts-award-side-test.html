Self-review — 4 perspectives

1. Corporate identity designer
  • A: Identity clear at top. Contacts balanced at 185px; award at 140px fits naturally as a credential badge. Hierarchy: name → contacts → award. Clean.
  • B: Contacts at 195px feel generous — almost too much room for 3 short lines. Award at 130px risks looking like an afterthought rather than a credential.
  • C: Award at 150px is slightly prominent relative to contacts at 175px — starts to unbalance the visual weight toward the award again.
  • Winner for identity: A. Closest to equal visual weight between the two zones.
2. Premium email signature designer
  • A: Award at 140px is large enough for the FT badge text to remain legible without feeling hero-sized. Good. Contact text at 13px fits the 185px column without wrapping for typical CDC addresses.
  • B: Award at 130px is borderline — the FT wordmark and ranking text become hard to read at small sizes. Loses premium feel.
  • C: Award at 150px is clearly legible and feels intentional. But the contact column at 175px feels tight if a real user has a longer email address (e.g. firstname.lastname@cdc.company → ~26 chars ≈ 195px at 13px, which would wrap).
  • Winner for premium: A or C. A is safer; C is only viable for short-address users.
3. iPhone Mail compatibility specialist
  • A: 185px contact column is sufficient for all typical CDC email patterns without wrapping. white-space:nowrap on contact links + 185px leaves ~158px of text space (185−22icon−5gap). Safe for most real users.
  • B: 195px contact column is the most conservative choice for truncation risk. Safest for users with long email addresses. Award at 130px is small but functional.
  • C: Contact column at 175px gives ~148px of text space. A long email like firstname.lastname@cdc.company (≈195px) would overflow horizontally. Risk of clipping or wrapping on iPhone.
  • Winner for compatibility: B. Safest. A is acceptable. C has truncation risk with long addresses.
4. UX designer
  • A: Best information hierarchy. The eye reads: identity → contacts (primary action items) → award (context). The award is visible but not competing. Address and disclaimer follow naturally. Not cramped.
  • B: The extra 10px on the contact side is not noticeable in practice. The shrinkage to 130px makes the award so small it starts to feel like filler rather than a credential. Slightly worse UX outcome despite being technically safer.
  • C: Award at 150px starts to pull the eye again — the contact→award ratio feels like the award is asking for more attention than the contacts. Hierarchy weakens slightly.
  • Winner for UX: A. Best balance of reading order, visual weight, and breathing room.
Recommendation: Candidate A (contacts 185px / award image 140px).

Candidate A wins 3 of 4 perspectives outright and ties on the fourth. The 185px contact column gives ample room for typical CDC email addresses without wrapping, while the 140px award image is clearly legible and sits as a natural credential badge rather than dominating the signature. Candidate B is the fallback if real-device testing shows truncation with long email addresses (safe choice but weaker visually). Candidate C introduces award-dominance and contact-truncation risk simultaneously — least recommended.

Recommended for iPhone testing: Candidate A. Open this file on iPhone in Safari → long-press the Candidate A signature → Copy → Settings → Mail → Signature → paste. Verify white background in both light and dark iOS themes, and confirm no horizontal clipping.
